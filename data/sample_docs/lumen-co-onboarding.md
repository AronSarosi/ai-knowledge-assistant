# New Starter Onboarding (2026)

*This is a sample document, part of a small set the assistant searches. The company
and the details below are fictional.*

## Welcome

Welcome to the company This guide walks you through your first few weeks so you
always know what happens next and who to ask. Nobody expects you to know
everything on day one, or even in your first month. Your job at the start is to
get set up, meet people, and ask plenty of questions; everything else follows from
there.

If anything in this guide is out of date or unclear, tell the People team. New
starters spot the gaps better than anyone, and we would rather fix the guide than
have you guess.

## Before your first day

A few days before you start, IT posts your laptop to you (or has it waiting in the
office, if you have chosen an office-first first day) along with a printed sheet of
first-day instructions. People will email you a short welcome pack: where to go,
who to ask for, what time to arrive, and a link to set up your accounts.

Please set up your password manager and multi-factor authentication before day one
if you can. The IT Security Policy explains how, and getting it done early means
your first morning is about meeting people rather than fighting with logins.

## Your first day

Aim to arrive at the office for 9:30am on your first day, a little later than the
normal start, so the team is already in and ready to greet you. Ask for the People
team at reception; they will be expecting you. Your manager and your onboarding
buddy will meet you, show you around, and take you for a coffee.

The morning is for setup: getting into your accounts, a tour, and a relaxed run
through this guide. The afternoon is lighter on purpose. You will sit in on one
real meeting just to listen, meet your immediate team, and finish early if you
like. There is no work expected of you on day one.

## Your onboarding buddy

Every new starter is paired with an onboarding buddy: an experienced colleague,
usually from a different team, who is your go-to for all the small questions that
feel too minor to bother your manager with. Where is the good coffee, how do we
write up a design review, who do I ask about payroll - that is buddy territory.

Your buddy checks in with you daily for the first week and weekly after that for
your first two months. They are not your manager and nothing you ask them is
reported up; they are simply there to make the start easier.

## Your first week

The first week is about meeting people and getting your bearings, not output. Your
manager will set up short introductory chats with everyone you will work closely
with, and you will shadow a couple of live projects to see how we actually work.

By the end of the week you should have access to every tool you need, have met your
team, and have a rough picture of what your first project will be. If you are
missing access to anything, tell IT and your buddy; do not sit blocked.

## Your first month and probation

In your first month your manager will agree three or four simple goals with you, so
"settling in" has something concrete behind it. You will have a 1:1 with your
manager every week. These are your time: bring whatever is on your mind, from the
work itself to how you are finding the place.

As explained in the Employee Handbook, new starters serve a six-month probation
period. You will get a clear, honest check-in at one month and three months, with
no surprises saved up for the end. If anything is not going well, you will hear it
early and with support to put it right.

## Tools and accounts

On day one you receive a laptop, an email account, access to the design tools and
code repositories, the password manager, and an invite to the team chat. Your buddy
will point you at the handful of channels worth joining first so you are not
drowning in notifications.

## Where to find things

Company policies, including the Employee Handbook and the IT Security Policy, live
in the People portal. Anything about pay or holiday is in the portal too. If you
cannot find something, ask your buddy or the People team - and if it turns out the
thing genuinely is not written down anywhere, that is worth flagging so we can fix
it for the next person.
